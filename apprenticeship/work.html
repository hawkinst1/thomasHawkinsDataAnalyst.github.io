Building a Data Analytic Service

18th September 2023 I began the route into Data Analytics within Gateway Housing Association, under the tutelage of long term analyst who had come from the Government (NZ). The task was to create a data analytics system that could replace/improve how internal and external data was handled, including:

  • Ccompliance to the NFD
  • General Data Protection Regulation
  • KPIs (internally)

This blog is a record of my time spent at Gateway and my journey going from complete beginner to a full industrial portfolio over 24 months.

What data to pick

When examining Youtube and other courses relating to data analysis, the emphasis is on the techniques but as a Beginner when faced with a large amount of data I get stuck with deciding what to even do relating to the above tasks.

To address this issue I have compiled a list of steps I take:

  • Define the Objectives - What are the goals of the analysis?
  • Relevance to the Objective - Only examine what will inform the obective.
  • KPIs - Try and identify the KPIs for your objective.
  • Varied Data - Constant data won't reveal trends.
  • Compare - Choose data that can be compared over time and across groups.
  • Granularity - Should your data be aggregated or granular (months vs days)

Example

Customer purchasing behaviour for a retail company

  • Define the objectives - Analyse the customers spending behaviour to optimise marketing
  • Relevant data - Transcation data, purchase dates, purchased items, purchase amount, associated discounts
  • KPIs - Average purchase amount, frequency of purchases per customer, top-selling products, conversion rates from promotion emails.
  • Varied Data - Data pf various purchase amounts, product types, and customer segments.
  • Compare - Purchase behaviour between customer segments such as new or returning customers
  • Granularity - For a longer time comparison we will use monthly data

Data exists everywhere now, regardless of sector and understanding the main types of data that can be found in the world is critical for its manipulation without error.

  • Qualitative
    • Nominal data
    • Ordinal data
  • Quantitative
    • Discrete data
    • Continuous data

Qualitative data can't be measured numerically, instead it represents categories and also contains audio and images found in databases.

Data: Time Series

Time-Series Analysis (TSA)

The specific analysis of sequential data points collected over time, generally the points are collected in a consistent time interval.

The objectives of carrying out a time analysis:

  • To understand what affect variables have at certain time points.
  • Supporting to derive a future result's prediction.
  • Assumption: "That the origin of time does not affect the properties of the process under the statisical factor" - Analytics Vidhya

A TSA is the backbone of prediction and forecasting analysis for time based statements and generally can be used for four componenets:

Trend: With no fixed interval in a continuous timeline a trend can be positive, negative or null

Seasonality: Regular or fixed interval shifts should see a bell curve at certain points.

Cyclical: No fixed intervals we expect sin wave.

Irregularities: Spikes from unforseen scenarios in a short time span.

Data types of Time Series

Firstly we need to distinguish between the major types which are stationary and non-stationary, if the mean, variance or covariance change respective to time then the data is non-stationary.

The mean is the average of the y-axis over time, the variance is the measurement of distance from the mean and covariance is the direction of a two variable relationship.

Moving Averages Methodology

An important aspect of TSA and forecasting, they serve to smooth out fluctuations and better reveal underlying trends in datasets. A Simple Moving Average (SMA) is the average of fixed points within a specified time window and equally wieghs all data points. A very simple way to reduce noise and identify trends, but is not able to respond to sudden changes in data. Weighted Moving Average (WMA) is similar to SMA but assigns different wieghts to the data points, which allows for emphasis on certain data points. Finally, Exponential Moving Averages (EMA) place greater wieght on the more recent data points and are able to quickly respond to changes.

Data: Part-to-whole

Part-To-Whole

A part-to-whole visualisation is best used for showing compositional comparisons and are best used for data that can be grouped into categories to determine their ratio of the whole set.
For viewing a 100% breakdown we will select Pie or Doughnut charts but it is also possible to view using stacked charts

Pie and Doughnut charts are simple, but also easy to make unreadable so I'll go over how cleaning it up should be done, according to: CultureCounts
  1. Try to keep the categories amount low, grouping them into 'other' until only 3 or 4 distinct categories remain.
  2. Try to keep the decreasing slices moving clockwise.
  3. If one segment is the priority of the report, highlight it with a distinct colour.

Deviation

The comparison of subdivisions against a reference to show the difference between the two, I couldnt find much on this and Standard Deviation seems to crop up a lot so I will take a look at the use of it in statistics.

Standard deviation is used for understanding the variablilty of a dataset and determining outliers of it. The higher the sd, larger the spread of values around the mean and the higer the variance of the data can be said to be.

data: Frequency Distribution

Frequency distribution

"A description of the number of observations for each possible value of a given variable, presented as graphs or tabulated" - Scribbr There are several types of distributions:

  • Ungrouped: Each value of a variable used for categories i.e., Menu items order amounts.
  • Grouped: A tally of a range of values i.e., frequency of 19-24 or 24-29 age groups in a survey.
  • Relative: A third column added which divides each frequency by the total to determine the relative frequency
  • Cumulative: Ungrouped or Relative table, a third column is used to sum at each row (the end row should be the total of all considered)

Graphical reprsentations of frequency distributions are Pie and Bar charts and Histograms.

Nominal Comparison

Nominal data is categorical or quantilative, and itself cannot be used in numerical operations, this would include data on hair colours, gender, nationality etc. Whilst there is no meaningful order; their values are distributed as distinct.

The testing availble for nominal datasets includes testing hypotheses via non-parametric tests with the most common being: Chi-squared. This tests for a significant difference between an expected frequency and the actual frequency of distinct categories.

Correlation

Probably the largest in terms of relevant tests that I have seen at a beginner's level, the main problem is avoiding the Correlation does not equal Causation saying. This can be done with three main tests on the data once plotted:

  • Pearson (default in Jupyter)

    A measure of the strength and the direction of a linear relationship between two variables, and relies on 4 assumptions:

    • Data is not ordnial or nominal
    • Linear data only can be tested for correlation
    • Outliers have been removed
    • Data is parametric

  • Spearman

    Relying on similar assumptions to Pearson, the only exception is it allows for ordinal data and can be done for non-parametric data.

  • Kendall

    Also able to be done on non-parametric data, it looks at dependencies between two variables.

All gathered from University of Illinois at Chicago - Link

For observing relationships between variables the two easiest to visually use are: Scatter plots and Heatmaps, with heatmaps having the potential for displaying non-numerical variables as well.

The errors and critical thinking of data analysis

Common Errors and their fixes

  • Selection Bias

    Problem: Skewed data from a non-reprsentative data pool.
    Fix: Selecting data from random sources.

  • Confirmation Bias

    Problem: Cherry picked data which ignores an outcome.
    Fix: Maintaining a neutral approach to your hypotheses.

  • Overfitting

    Problem: Statistical model trained with noise present leading to real-life failure.
    Fix: Noise smoothing techniques such as SMA discussed in earlier post.

  • Cleaning Errors

    Problem: Distorted results
    Fix: Thorough approach to how the cleaning is carried out in the process.
